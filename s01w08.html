// 05.10.21 - MIT Media Lab, Media Arts & Sciences Open House.

Saw that the MIT Media Arts & Sciences Graduate Program, MIT Media Lab, was having their online open house and decided to attend to listen in on what's going on in the lab as there might be concepts and ideas that were related to my research. I've also gotten interested in maybe applying for the program to do my masters in the future so it would be good knowledge to find out more about them. Although I'm not too sure if I stand a chance but still might try.

I was particularly interested in the research group doing presentations on their research and was particularly interested in four groups. The first group would be the Affective Computing Group which is the group where my research stemmed from, but unfortunately they weren't presenting this year as they're not currently accepting students.

Image: Hiroshi Ishii showing inFORM.

The second group, with some relation to my research, was the Tangible Media Group, where the boss man Hiroshi Ishii gave a presentation on the focus of their group where they focused on changing the way people interact with digital information making it more tangible, something interesting from his presentation was that he briefly mentioned the concept of "Tangible Telepresence" and he showed the projec inFORM where the idea was to create a future beyond zoom(video calls), providing means of Tangible Telepresence.

Images: Tangible Media Group's philosophy.

He also shared the philosophy of the Tangible Media Group and I thought it was quite interesting where it was like finding the balance between art and science? (Well duh, the program is Media Art and Science), but something about being poetic is a recurring thing in this intersection. The spiral he shared which he referred to as their critical engine for creation was also interesting to see the process behind their thoughts and how factors of each discipline link with each other. And it makes a lot of sense, art kind of shapes the research, science explains it, technology makes it possible and then through design a solution appears.

Images: Future Sketches Group's philosophy.

The next group that I was interested in was the Future Sketches Group, the group was headed up by Zach Lieberman and was a relatively new group at the Media Lab compared to the other groups. This was also the group that I was most likely to apply to, if I was applying and design tools have always been an interest of mine. Also because I've been a fan of Lieberman's work and his previous school, School For Poetic Computation.

Images: Research questions asked by the Future Sketches Group.

Once again the idea of poetic computation is a focus here, interesting, might be time to dive into what exactly poetic computation is. But I thought what the group did was quite interesting as Lieberman mentioned that the group exists at the intersection of computation, art and design and asks the question of what are the tools and kits we should be developing for expression that responds to and augments the body. So what do the next generation of tool kits look like? Could they introduce new interactions? Utilize Machine Learning? Be mobile focused? And how can artistic intervention shape our thinking around key technologies? What are the artistic applications? Can we make poems out of demos? What are the reusable components in tools? How can we bridge technologies?

Images: Fluid Interfaces Group's philosophy.

One group that I did not know about before this was the Fluid Interface Group but I found their presentation really interesting. The group focuses on designing systems for cognitive support, which I found might provide some insight into my research as well. Although their approach was more based in neuroscience and engineering. But there is something to be learnt from their approach.

Images: Fluid Interfaces Group's philosophy.

The idea of technology assisted living was quite interesting as it looked at key technologies that I was also looking into. The cycle where they take insights to develop systems to enable opportunities which in turn provide new insights was close to the iterative design cycle which I am using for my research, so that was quite cool to see that a similar framework was being used.

Overall this was quite a fruitful session, although it lasted from 1am till 4am and I'm not sure how much I managed to absorb, but I believe I managed to get some valuable insights and it was really interesting to see different types of research going on within the Media Lab. Here's hoping that I will have a portfolio that could stand a chance in applying as a masters candidate in the future.

// 06.10.21 - 08.10.21 - Arduino Exploration: Neuewave, Sea of Debris.

Short break from the regularly scheduled research related explorations. Dhiya, my intern supervisor from Neuewave, approached me to see if I was interested in helping with the electronics for a piece, Sea of Debris, that he was working on for this year's Archifest. I thought the idea was quite interesting, and agreed to work with him on it as it is an opportunity for me to learn how to work with Linear Actuators and further my programming competency while working on a pretty cool project at the same time. I believe that this adds to my technological confidence and it's a part of my learning journey which is why I'm sharing my explorations here in the CPJ.

The end goal of the project was to control four Linear Actuators, getting them to move in either a sinusoidal wave manner or based on a perlin noise wave pattern, with sensors attached to create certain interruptions or influence movement in a different direction. So I broke down the tasks to first figuring out how to get the Linear Actuators to work, then how to get sensors to control them before figuring out how to get it to move based on a fixed pattern. I'll work with one Linear Actuator at first before I scale it up to four.

// 08.10.21 - Arduino Exploration: Linear Actuator Fixed Movement.

First things first getting the Linear Actuator to move. There were two ways to do this, one using analogWrite to give it a value, while the other was digitalWrite to give it a "HIGH" or "LOW" output. I was trying to figure out the difference between the two when someone on discord helped explain it to me.

Something funny that happened was at one point the Linear Actuator was only extending a short length instead of the full length and I stared at the code for a good five minutes thinking there was something wrong there, but as it turns out, I forgot to turn on the 5V power source. Major fail. I also attempted to power two Linear Actuators and the code looks like it would work, but it didn't? After talking to Dhiya about it, we figured it might be because we were using a 5V power source when we needed a 12V source, which we were still in the process of acquiring, so for testing purposes, we will be sticking to just a single Linear Actuator for now.

Parts List:


1x Arduino Uno REV3
1x L298N Motor Drive
1x Linear Actuator
Jumper Wires

// 08.10.21 - Arduino Exploration: PIR Sensor Trigger Linear Actuator.

With the sensor working, the next step was hooking it up to the Motor Drive and the Linear Actuator. Everything works fine, but what I realised on further testing was that the sensor was quite finicky and sometimes would not detect the correct thing. My hand would be moving but it would detect no movement, or my hand would not be over the sensor but it will detect movement, although the rate of that happening is relatively low, it is still something to note and it might be better to look at some other sensor options.
