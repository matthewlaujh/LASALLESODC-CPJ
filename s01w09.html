It could also be applied to different kinds of materials and that could be interesting. So I decided to borrow the paint as well as some blocks of wood and see what I could do for this prototyping workshop.

The first part of this was trying to figure out how to use the paint, so in class, using the pre painted board that Andreas had, I used the code provided by Bare Conductive and at first it was not working, but Andreas helped figure things out, there was something weird going on with different baud rates which was causing the readings to be inaccurate, but that was resolved with some help. Thomas and I were talking in class and we were once again saying "it's probably something really obvious but we just can't figure it out", turns out when Andreas came over, it really was. but all part of the learning process, now we know a bit more about baud rates.

Wrapping up this side project that I worked on with my internship company Neuewave, progressing on from part one last week, this time around I had the right power supply to provide power to the actuator as well as an IR distance sensor to play with. So having the right power supply was definitely a "game-changer" in a sense. The difference in the speed and force of the actuator is quite noticeable. So this is something to note if I decide to use actuators and motors as some form of output in my project down the road. The next thing was testing out the IR distance sensor, this was something that was causing so, so, so very many issues. Firstly, I didn't have any experience with them, so I was just using rough values to just "guestimate" what would be the right values, also I had two sensors, and I didn't realise that they had different ranges. So that's on me to learn to double check the hardware when someone passes them to you and to not take it for granted. So after figuring that out, I just did a quick sensor test code, to get a good gauge of the distance and their values (that made things so much easier). But then another problem arose, the sensor was picking up random "bursts" of values, even when there was nothing in front of it. That caused the whole system to go a bit crazy, the hypothesis, was that it was either random small "bursts" of electricity causing it to go haywire or it could have been the sensors were a little old and used (as they have been used in other projects before), so they were already a bit wonky, or it might've just been something to do with the code.

So as there wasn't really any way to test the first two hypotheses (due to lack of time and resources), I decided just to mess around with the code a little, playing around with the if/else conditional statements to see if that would make a difference, well it did not. But I guess it was worth the time just to get some more experience with conditional statements, just having different ways to write code to do similar actions. So due to lack of time, we decided to move on with the project just programming a fixed motion instead of relying on sensor data.

So next up was building "The Brain", the circuit had to be presented cleanly as it was exposed, we couldn't have random wires running around in a mess, that would've been very unpresentable and ugly. The object we built was also very long, so I had to make some cables myself to fit the length of the frame, so that the actuators could go on to each corner. This was a good exercise in learning how to sort of "present" the circuits as well as learning sort of the DIY methods of making your own cables, basically cutting up jumper wires and then reattaching them to longer cables at each end so that you still have the connector part. Although this tended to get quite frustrating as well cause my fingers weren't nimble enough to hook the raw exposed cables together, and I ended up ripping apart more of them than I put together at the start, and also at times, I would connect the wires together, but my pea brain would forget to slip in the heat shrink in before that, so I had to strip them again, so that was actually more work, but towards the end I got a hang of things and it actually went on quite smoothly.

With the brain assembled, we hooked it up to the frame and tested to see how everything worked together. First thing we realised was that, although the actuators were moving, it wasn't giving us the intended effect. It was supposed to generate enough height to let gravity do it's thing and then the beads and marbles would sway from side to side to create a nice visual and sound. But the actuators weren't going high enough to let generate enough momentum, so we had to reduce the amount of beads and increase the marbles to get them to move. The issue with the beads were that there was a small part of it where there's a flat surface so it wasn't entirely round and they were quite light, so they occasionally would clump together and then stop moving. So the marbles were a better option as they were completely round and had a certain weight to them. So we had to find the right mix of beads and marbles as well a program the actuators in a way that would generate enough momentum to keep the beads and marbles moving. Took me some on the spot adjusting of the duration of motors moving as well as the order of them moving to generate the right amount of momentum but eventually we figured out something that worked.

So anyways some final thoughts on this side projected that I worked on, I think despite the challenges faced, it did help me learn quite a few different things. Firstly I think the most important thing was that, the idea and concept behind the work was really important, the tech and the tools were just a means to getting it to work. That's something that I've forgotten more often than not throughout this year. The next thing is to always understand and check the hardware that you're working with. That would help save a lot of unnecessary troubleshooting. Find ways to present circuits neatly. Even if it's just a simple acrylic base, it would help with the overall presentation. It was good that I got a chance to DIY make some cables, that should come in handy later on. Learning to work with Motors and Actuators would help with possible creative outputs later on, it also increased my competency and confidence working with those hardwares and figuring out how to get them to work. Probably a good idea not to save money on spending on the necessary and the right components, the whole issue with the beads and marbles could have been avoided if we had actuators that extended just a few cm longer, but we opted for the cheaper shorter actuators instead and that caused a bunch of issues. As mentioned in my intern report, something Dhiya always mentioned was Murphy's Law, that did come into play once during this project when one of the actuators failed to move 3 days before the set up, but luckily I reminded Dhiya to order one extra one, a week before and it arrived one day before we had to set up. So it's probably best to get some spare parts earlier next time to avoid this panic.
